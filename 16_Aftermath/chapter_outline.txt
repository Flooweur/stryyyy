CHAPTER 16: AFTERMATH (CLOSING IMAGE)
======================================

CHAPTER PITCH:
Six months after Simera's death, Liz sits in a new apartment paid for with the blood money from the Memento sale, surrounded by the modest comfort that 250,000 units can buy, and as she contemplates the neural connector she's kept hidden in a drawer—the device that would let her experience the Memento of Simera's final fall, the bestselling death in Heri's history that she hasn't yet consumed—she realizes with creeping horror that the system has made her both mourner and potential consumer of her own girlfriend's murder, and in the novel's final scene she walks past a Heri Corporation building where she sees a young woman, thin and desperate-looking, entering for what Liz recognizes immediately as a creator recruitment appointment, and as Liz opens her mouth to warn her, to tell her to run, to explain how the system will consume her, she finds herself saying nothing, just walking past, because she understands now that the machine is too large, too entrenched, too hungry to stop, that for every Simera who dies there are a dozen more waiting to fall, and she returns home to her comfortable apartment built on death, sits on her new couch, and stares at the drawer containing the Memento connector, knowing she will eventually use it, eventually consume Simera's death like thousands of others have, because grief and addiction and consumption blur together until there's no difference, until we're all falling, all the time, forever.

FINAL PARAGRAPHS (SUGGESTED CONTENT):

Liz sits on the couch, connector in hand. The city beyond her window pulses with life—people working, consuming, falling, dying, repeating. Somewhere in that vast machine, another desperate person is climbing stairs to a roof. Somewhere, someone is attaching a neural connector to experience death they didn't live. Somewhere, Heri Corporation is processing applications, scheduling recordings, calculating profit margins. The system eats. The system grows. The system continues.

She thinks of Simera—both versions. The one who chose death clearly, articulating her reasons through the fog of brain injury. The one who died screaming, desperately wanting life. Which was real? Which was right? She doesn't know. Will never know. All she knows: both are gone. Both consumed. Both sold. 180,000 times over.

The connector is warm in her palm. She could put it down. Walk away. Refuse to participate further. Let Simera rest. Let the dead stay dead instead of experiencing their dying over and over through consumer demand. She should put it down.

Should.

But grief is heavy, and the apartment is empty, and somewhere in this small device is Simera—not Simera herself, but an echo, a shadow, a recording of her final moments. And Liz has been alone for six months and will be alone for decades more and everyone is always alone, really, aren't they? Except for these moments, these borrowed experiences, these Mementos that let you be someone else, feel something else, die someone else's death because your own life is unbearable.

She understands Simera now. Truly understands. The poverty was symptom. The falling was symptom. The real disease was something deeper, something no money could cure: the unbearable weight of being yourself, conscious, alone, until death. And if death is the only exit, why not experience it? Practice it? Consume it? Until the practicing becomes the real thing?

Outside her window, the city falls. Not literally. Not yet. But everyone is falling, all the time, toward their own inevitable ground. Some fall faster. Some fall consciously. Some create their falls for others to consume. But everyone falls.

Liz's hand tightens on the connector.

Just this once, she thinks. Just to remember. Just to understand. Just to feel close to her one more time. Just this once.

The same words. Everyone's words. The system's words, spoken through everyone's mouths.

She lifts the connector toward the port behind her ear.

The city falls.
Everyone falls.
Forever falling.
Never landing.

Except when they do.

And then: sold.

[THE END]
